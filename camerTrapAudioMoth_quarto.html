TBS Species Distribution Based on Camera Trap Sightings

How species respond to risk is in part determined by the distribution of predators within their environment. Spatial heterogeneity in the distribution of predation causes variation in how animals perceive risk across the landscape. I deployed a grid of camera traps paired with autonomous acoustic monitoring devices at 20 monitoring stations to investigate the distribution of terrestrial predators across the landscape. In doing so, I aimed to asses if and how lowland woolly monkeys perceive and respond to different levels of risk across their environment. Monitor stations were placed at roughly 250 meter intervals from each other, with 5 stations running east to west and 4 running north to south, covering an area of an estimate 125 hectares.

Camera traps were able to document the presence of terrestrial predators and numerous prey species. Habitual use of particular locations by prey can be used as an indicator of potentially “safe” areas where risk is perceived as low.

This document displays the distribution of camera trap sightings at three levels: (1) the number of times a species was captures at a monitor station (2) the number of sightings of a species each hour per day and (3) the number of days a monitor station captured a species at least once. I constructed heat maps based on the rate of captures of each species to identify potential hot spots of activity across the landscape.
